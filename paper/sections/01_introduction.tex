\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Pick a model by reading the top of an accuracy leaderboard and you
miss most of what matters for statistical reasoning. Accuracy fuses
three things a downstream user cares about separately: did the model
land on the right number, did it still land on the right number when
the prompt was reworded or the numbers swapped, and does its stated
confidence track its empirical correctness. StatEval~\cite{lu2025stateval},
MathEval~\cite{liu2025matheval}, Nagarkar et al.~\cite{nagarkar2026canllm},
and ad-hoc Bayesian evaluations~\cite{longjohn2025bayesian} all
report accuracy alone. When the three properties disagree, that
column is misleading.

We make the disagreement concrete on 171 Bayesian and inferential
tasks. Five frontier LLMs are scored on accuracy, robustness, and
calibration; the rank orders differ. Two patterns carry the
headline. GPT-4.1 is last on accuracy (0.621) but first on robustness
(0.9996); its base and perturbation pass rates fall within 0.0003 of
each other. Claude leads accuracy (0.683) and binary-pass calibration
(ECE 0.0334) yet drops to fourth on robustness (0.9565), losing three
points between base and perturbed prompts. A reader who only sees
the accuracy column gets neither pattern. 44.3\% of responses fail
to state the assumptions the derivation requires, even when the
final number is correct; assumption violation accounts for 47\% of
judge-classified failures. Du et al.~\cite{du2025icecream} report
the same blindness in causal reasoning.

The methodological gap behind the headline is worth naming. Most
LLM-statistics evaluations rely on a keyword rubric, a single judge,
or both. Both have known biases. Keyword rubrics confuse surface
form with substance: the word ``assume'' is not the same as actually
stating an assumption. Single-judge protocols suffer self-preference
bias and design-choice sensitivity~\cite{park2025judge,judgment2025noise}.
We score each response twice: once with a deterministic keyword
rubric, once with an external out-of-family judge (Llama 3.3 70B
Turbo via Together AI, not one of the five benchmarked models). The
two views disagree where it matters. They agree only moderately on
assumption-compliance (Spearman 0.602) and barely at all on method
structure and reasoning quality. The rubric also passes assumptions
more leniently than the judge (71.1\% vs 55.7\%), which is why
keyword-only protocols read Bayesian competence higher than it is.

Contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A benchmark of 171 Bayesian and inferential tasks (136
        closed-form conjugate models; 35 computational methods
        covering Gibbs, Metropolis-Hastings, HMC, RJMCMC, hierarchical,
        VB, ABC) with 473 perturbations on three axes covering all
        38 task types.
  \item An external-judge protocol using an out-of-family open-weights
        model. Keyword-vs-judge agreement is reported per dimension
        (Spearman 0.602 on assumption-compliance, much lower
        elsewhere). The gap between what a rubric sees and what a
        judge sees is itself a finding.
  \item Empirical evidence that accuracy, robustness, and calibration
        rankings of the same five LLMs disagree non-trivially: a
        5-to-1 inversion for GPT-4.1 and a 1, 1, 4 pattern for
        Claude. Neither shows up on a single-metric leaderboard.
  \item An open audit trail. NMACR weights (A=0.30, R=0.25, M=0.20,
        C=0.15, N=0.10), aggregation choices, and tolerance
        thresholds are documented and tied to the papers that
        motivated them.
\end{enumerate}
