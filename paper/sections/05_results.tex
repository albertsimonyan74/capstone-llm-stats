\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Per-Metric Rankings}

Table~\ref{tab:rankings} shows the per-model summary across the three
metrics. Accuracy is the Path~B NMACR-weighted pass rate on the 171
base tasks; robustness is one minus the perturbation pass-flip rate
on the 473 perturbations; ECE is binary-pass Expected Calibration
Error (lower is better).

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{Per-Model Performance Across the Three Metrics}
\label{tab:rankings}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Model} & \textbf{Accuracy $\uparrow$} & \textbf{Robustness $\uparrow$} & \textbf{ECE $\downarrow$} \\
\midrule
Claude Sonnet 4.5 & \textbf{0.683} & 0.9565 & \textbf{0.0334} \\
Mistral Large     & 0.644 & 0.9981 & 0.0811 \\
Gemini 2.5 Flash  & 0.642 & 0.9824 & 0.0765 \\
DeepSeek-Chat     & 0.625 & 0.9422 & 0.1977 \\
GPT-4.1           & 0.621 & \textbf{0.9996} & 0.0339 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

No two of the three rankings agree. The accuracy ranking is
Claude $>$ Mistral $>$ Gemini $>$ DeepSeek $>$ GPT-4.1, with Claude
ahead of the runner-up by nearly four percentage points and GPT-4.1
in last place. The robustness ranking is
GPT-4.1 $>$ Mistral $>$ Gemini $>$ Claude $>$ DeepSeek, which moves
GPT-4.1 from last to first and Claude from first to fourth. The
calibration ranking is
Claude $>$ GPT-4.1 $>$ Gemini $>$ Mistral $>$ DeepSeek; Claude and
GPT-4.1 are essentially tied at the top (the 0.0005 gap is within the
95\% bootstrap CI estimated over 10{,}000 resamples), while DeepSeek
is the clear laggard at ECE $0.198$, six times the leader's error.
DeepSeek is the only model that consistently ranks among the bottom
two on every axis.

\subsection{Headline Divergence}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/three_rankings.png}
  \caption{Per-model performance across the three metrics. Rankings
           differ: the accuracy-best model is not the robustness-best
           (GPT-4.1 inversion); the accuracy-and-calibration leader
           places fourth on robustness (Claude pattern).}
  \label{fig:three-rankings}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:three-rankings} visualizes the rank divergence. Two
parallel examples carry the headline. \textbf{Example 1 (the GPT-4.1
inversion).} The model that finishes last on accuracy (GPT-4.1,
$0.621$) finishes first on robustness ($0.9996$), with an essentially
zero gap between its base and perturbation pass rates ($0.6661$ vs
$0.6658$). \textbf{Example 2 (the Claude pattern).} The model that
leads on accuracy ($0.683$) and is tied for the lead on calibration
(ECE $0.0334$) places fourth on robustness ($0.9565$), with a three-
percentage-point drop between base and perturbation pass rates
($0.7011$ vs $0.6706$). Neither pattern is visible from a single
accuracy column. The configurations they instantiate ---
high-accuracy-low-robustness and low-accuracy-high-robustness ---
are usually treated as theoretical edge cases; here they are the
empirical centerpieces of the same five-model leaderboard. A consumer
choosing GPT-4.1 because of its robustness score would be choosing
the model with the lowest base accuracy on the benchmark; a consumer
choosing Claude because of its accuracy and calibration scores would
be choosing the third most brittle of the five.

\subsection{Assumption-Compliance Results}

The external Llama 3.3 70B Turbo judge scored each (task, response)
pair on whether the model explicitly stated the statistical
assumptions the task required. Across $n=750$ paired runs, the judge
passes only $55.7\%$ of responses on the assumption-compliance
dimension --- meaning $44.3\%$ of responses fail to articulate the
assumptions the derivations depend on, even when the final numeric
answer is correct. The keyword rubric, by contrast, passes $71.1\%$
of the same responses on the same dimension, an absolute gap of
$15.4$ percentage points. Spearman rank correlation between keyword
and judge scores on assumption-compliance is $\rho = 0.602$
(95\% CI via Krippendorff bootstrap $[0.516, 0.622]$).

On the larger combined pool ($n=2850$ eligible runs, base $+$
perturbation), $20.7\%$ of responses exhibit a pass-flip between the
keyword rubric and the judge --- the keyword rubric and the judge
disagree on pass/fail status. This gap is the strongest single piece
of evidence for the title's reasoning-versus-pattern-matching framing:
models produce surface-correct outputs that pass keyword checks for
``assume'' or ``given'' tokens while failing the substantive check
of whether the assumption is actually articulated. The
assumption-blindness pattern matches the causal-reasoning failure
mode catalogued by Du et al.~\cite{du2025icecream}.

\subsection{Per-Dimension Calibration}

Krippendorff's $\alpha$ on the three judged dimensions (method
structure, assumption compliance, reasoning quality) sits in the
``questionable'' range: $-0.009$, $0.573$, and $-0.125$ respectively.
Assumption-compliance is the only dimension with non-trivial
inter-rater reliability, and even there the $\alpha = 0.573$ is below
the conventional $0.667$ cutoff for tentative conclusions. This is
honest disclosure rather than a refutation of our central claim ---
the three-ranking divergence is a within-model cross-metric finding,
not an inter-rater agreement claim --- and we revisit the
implications in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

Per-NMACR-dimension ECE tells a finer story than the binary-pass ECE
in Table~\ref{tab:rankings}. On the Reasoning Quality (R) dimension
specifically, GPT-4.1 leads with ECE $0.097$, followed by Mistral
($0.102$), DeepSeek ($0.106$), Claude ($0.117$), and Gemini ($0.130$).
The model that leads on overall binary-pass ECE (Claude) is third on
the R-dimension calibration, while the accuracy-bottom GPT-4.1 leads
both robustness and reasoning calibration. The picture matches the
binary-pass ECE story directionally but locates the GPT-4.1
``reasoning calibration'' advantage more precisely.
Figure~\ref{fig:dimension-leaderboard} visualises the per-dimension
breakdown across all five NMACR components.
