\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We benchmarked five frontier LLMs on 171 Bayesian and inferential
statistical tasks with 473 perturbations, scored by a deterministic
keyword rubric, a literature-weighted NMACR aggregator, and an
external out-of-family Llama 3.3 70B Turbo judge constrained to
assumption-compliance. The same five models produce three different
rank orders depending on the evaluation axis. The model with the
lowest base accuracy (GPT-4.1, $0.621$) is the most robust to
perturbation ($0.9996$); the model that leads on both accuracy
($0.683$) and binary-pass calibration (ECE $0.0334$) places fourth
of five on robustness ($0.9565$). Independent of ranking, $44.3\%$
of responses fail to articulate the statistical assumptions their
derivations require, and the keyword rubric overcounts assumption
articulation by $15.4$ percentage points relative to the external
judge. Single-number leaderboards are insufficient for
statistical-reasoning evaluation; the right ranking depends on the
property being measured.

\textbf{Threats to validity.} The Llama 3.3 70B Turbo judge may have
systematic biases inherited from its training, which we mitigate by
constraining its scope to one dimension rather than a holistic
preference signal and by validating against the keyword rubric
(Spearman $\rho = 0.602$ on assumption-compliance). Krippendorff's
$\alpha$ across the three judged dimensions falls in the
``questionable'' range; this reflects genuine ambiguity in scoring
free-form Bayesian reasoning and is reported here as honest
disclosure rather than instrument failure. The three-ranking
divergence is a within-model cross-metric finding and does not
require inter-rater agreement to hold. Task coverage is broad for
Bayesian and inferential reasoning but is not representative of
statistical reasoning generally --- causal inference and experimental
design are out of scope. Mean perturbation count is $2.77$ per base
task, and ECE estimates rely on $10{,}000$-sample bootstrap CIs that
capture the resulting uncertainty. Model versions are May 2026
snapshots and the inversion patterns may not replicate as APIs
evolve.

\textbf{Future work.} Three extensions follow naturally. First,
broaden the task suite into causal inference, experimental design,
and frequentist hypothesis testing to test whether the three-axis
divergence generalizes beyond Bayesian-specific reasoning. Second,
replicate the analysis with a second out-of-family judge to bound the
judge-bias variance; cross-judge agreement on assumption-compliance
would be a defensible upper bound on inter-rater reliability for the
dimension. Third, treat the GPT-4.1 accuracy-to-robustness inversion
as a hypothesis about training-data composition and decoding choices,
and design controlled experiments at the provider level that isolate
the relevant factors.
