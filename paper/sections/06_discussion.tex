\section{Analysis and Validation}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{What the three-ranking divergence means.} The three metrics
are not redundant restatements of an underlying ``quality'' axis.
Accuracy measures whether the model produces the right number for the
prompt as worded. Robustness measures whether the same model produces
the same answer when the prompt is reworded, the framing changes, or
the numbers shift. Calibration measures whether the model's stated
confidence tracks its empirical correctness. Each property has
deployment consequences that the other two do not capture, which is
why a single-number leaderboard is structurally insufficient: the
choice of which metric to report determines which model ``wins.''
For one-shot question answering, accuracy is the natural primary
signal. For applications where the user paraphrases the prompt or
substitutes equivalent numerical setups --- automated tutoring,
prompt-engineered pipelines, agentic workflows that synthesize their
own prompts --- robustness dominates, and the GPT-4.1 inversion
suggests that the accuracy-best model can be the wrong default.
For downstream decision systems that consume model outputs gated on
expressed confidence --- triage tools, ensemble selectors --- well-
calibrated confidence is the requirement, and DeepSeek's ECE of
$0.198$ disqualifies it from that role regardless of its accuracy
position.

\textbf{Reasoning versus pattern matching.} The strongest single
piece of evidence for the paper's titular question is the gap between
the keyword rubric and the external judge on assumption-compliance.
On the combined base-and-perturbation pool of $2850$ eligible
responses, $20.7\%$ show a pass-flip between the two scorers ---
roughly one in five responses that the keyword check approves are
rejected by the judge, or vice versa, on whether the required
assumption was actually articulated. The judge's lower pass rate
($55.7\%$ vs the rubric's $71.1\%$) implies that the keyword check is
the more lenient scorer, which is the relevant direction: surface-
form scoring overcounts substantive assumption articulation. The
finding matters because assumption articulation is not a stylistic
preference for Bayesian reasoning, it is the methodological core ---
posterior derivations are wrong if the assumptions are wrong, and a
model that lands on the right number while quietly assuming
exchangeability where the task posits hierarchical structure has
solved a different problem than the one asked. Du et
al.~\cite{du2025icecream} document the same blindness in causal
reasoning. A parallel divergence appears in the calibration analysis
(Figure~\ref{fig:calibration-paired}): verbalized confidence places
Claude and GPT-4.1 in the well-calibrated tier (ECE $\approx 0.033$),
while self-consistency confidence on three reruns at $T = 0.7$ pushes
every model past the $\text{ECE} = 0.6$ ``severe overconfidence''
threshold. Verbalized confidence is pattern matching on
calibration-shaped language; self-consistency probes the underlying
answer distribution. The implication for evaluation design is that
future benchmarks for statistical reasoning should treat
assumption-compliance as an independent dimension scored by a
non-self-referential judge, and should report calibration with both
verbalized and self-consistency extractors, not as a sub-clause of a
keyword rubric.

\textbf{Broader implications.} The multi-axis ranking framework is
inexpensive to add to existing benchmarks: the same response corpus
that supports an accuracy leaderboard supports a robustness leader-
board once perturbations exist, and a calibration leaderboard once
confidence is parsed. The GPT-4.1 accuracy-to-robustness inversion in
particular suggests that training-data composition and decoding
choices may trade off ``follows the textbook'' against ``answers the
specific question asked,'' an investigation that would require
controlled experiments at the provider level rather than black-box
benchmarking. Beyond the empirical finding, an explicit contribution
of this work is the open audit trail: every scoring weight, tolerance
threshold, and aggregation choice is documented and linked to the
literature that motivated it, so that future replication or
reweighting requires only a one-line code change rather than a
reverse-engineering effort. Single-number leaderboards remain useful
shorthand; we argue they should be accompanied, not replaced, by the
multi-axis view that surfaces the divergences they hide.
