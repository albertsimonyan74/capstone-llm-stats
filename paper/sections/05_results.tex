\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Per-Metric Rankings}

The per-model summary appears in Table~\ref{tab:per-model}. Accuracy
is the mean Path~B NMACR score on the 171 base tasks. Robustness
$R_m$ is the ratio of mean NMACR score on the $n=398$ paired
perturbation runs to mean NMACR score on their base counterparts
(Eq.~\ref{eq:robustness}), so $R_m = 1$ corresponds to perfect
base-to-perturbation agreement. ECE is binary-pass Expected
Calibration Error (lower is better).

No two rankings agree. Orderings, rank-flips, and 95\% bootstrap
CIs are detailed in \S\ref{sec:headline-divergence} below. DeepSeek
is the only model in the bottom two on every axis.

\subsection{Headline Divergence}
\label{sec:headline-divergence}

The cross-metric divergence is concrete: GPT-4.1 ranks third on
accuracy ($0.673$, behind Gemini $0.731$ and Claude $0.698$) yet
its base and perturbation mean NMACR scores fall within $0.0003$
($0.6661$ vs $0.6658$) --- first on robustness ($0.9996$). Claude
inverts: second on accuracy ($0.698$), tied with GPT-4.1 on
calibration (ECE $\approx 0.033$), but mean NMACR drops from
$0.7011$ to $0.6706$ under perturbation, dropping it to fourth on
robustness ($0.9565$).

\subsection{Assumption-Compliance Results}
\label{sec:assumption-compliance}

On $n=750$ paired runs (150 base tasks $\times$ 5 models, excluding
21 task specifications with no required assumption checks; distinct
from the 40-task numerical-axis filter in \S\ref{sec:methodology})
the Llama 3.3 70B Turbo judge passes only $55.7\%$ of responses on
assumption-compliance, leaving $44.3\%$
that fail to articulate the assumptions the derivation depends on,
even when the final number is right. The keyword rubric passes
$71.1\%$ of the same responses, $15.4$ points higher. Spearman
rank correlation between keyword and judge scores on this dimension
is $\rho = 0.602$; Krippendorff $\alpha = 0.573$ (95\% CI
$[0.516, 0.622]$, 1{,}000-resample bootstrap;
Table~\ref{tab:krippendorff-strip}).

On the combined base-plus-perturbation pool ($n=2{,}850$ = 750
base + 2{,}100 perturbation rows, eligibility scoped to tasks with
at least one required assumption check), $20.7\%$ of responses
pass-flip between the keyword rubric and the judge: one in five
times, the two scorers disagree on
whether the assumption was actually articulated. That is the
clearest evidence in the paper for the title's question. Models
produce surface-correct outputs that pass keyword checks for
``assume'' or ``given'' tokens while failing the substantive check.
Du et al.~\cite{du2025icecream} report the same blindness in causal
reasoning.

Figure~\ref{fig:disagreement-matrix} visualizes the direction of
the disagreement. Of 722 disagreeing runs in the $n=2{,}850$ pool,
591 are keyword-PASS / judge-FAIL versus only 131 the reverse --- a
$4.5\times$ asymmetry showing the rubric over-credits surface tokens
like ``assume'' or ``given'' without substantive assumption
articulation.

\subsection{Failure Taxonomy}
\label{sec:failure-taxonomy}

The judge tags each failing response with a Level-1 (L1) severity
flag when the response fails substantive assumption-compliance
independently of whether the final numeric answer is correct. The
143 L1 responses cluster into five categories.
Figure~\ref{fig:failure-taxonomy} breaks them down per model.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\columnwidth]{figures/failure_taxonomy.png}
  \caption{Failure-mode distribution across the five models
           ($n=143$ judge-classified L1 failures, stacked by
           category). Assumption violations dominate at $46.9\%$
           (67), then mathematical errors $33.6\%$ (48), formatting
           $12.6\%$ (18), and conceptual $7.0\%$ (10). \textbf{No
           hallucinations observed} on these tasks.}
  \label{fig:failure-taxonomy}
\end{figure}

Three observations follow. Assumption violations dominate,
reinforcing the keyword-vs-judge gap in
\S\ref{sec:assumption-compliance}: in Bayesian reasoning the
substantive failure mode is unstated or implicit assumptions, not
incorrect arithmetic. Hallucinations are absent --- fabrication is
widely cited as a general LLM risk, but on structured tasks with
explicit ground-truth anchors it does not appear here. Third, the
per-model breakdown sharpens the GPT-4.1 robustness paradox:
GPT-4.1 has the most overall L1 failures (38 of 143), of which 25
are assumption violations. The model ranking first on robustness
also omits assumption articulation most --- robustness here
reflects absence of articulated reasoning to vary, not stable
reasoning.

\subsection{Calibration Across Extraction Methods}

A single ECE number hides where the (mis)calibration comes from.
Two extraction methods are separated below. \emph{Verbalized confidence} uses
what the model states alongside its answer. \emph{Self-consistency
confidence} is inferred from how often the model returns the same
answer across reruns at temperature $T = 0.7$.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Calibration ECE by Two Extraction Methods}
  \label{tab:calibration-paired}
  \resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{%
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Model} & \textbf{Verbalized ECE $\downarrow$}
                   & \textbf{Self-consistency ECE $\downarrow$} \\
                   & {\footnotesize $n=855$ base runs}
                   & {\footnotesize $n=2{,}415$ reruns, $T=0.7$} \\
    \midrule
    Claude    & 0.033 & 0.734 \\
    GPT-4.1   & 0.034 & 0.721 \\
    Gemini    & 0.077 & 0.618 \\
    Mistral   & 0.081 & 0.663 \\
    DeepSeek  & 0.198 & 0.726 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}%
  }

  \vspace{2pt}
  \footnotesize{\textit{Note}: Left column = verbalized ECE on
  $n=855$ truly-base runs (all 171 base tasks $\times$ 5 models;
  calibration uses the full base pool with no assumption-check
  filter, distinct from the $n=750$ pool in
  \S\ref{sec:assumption-compliance}). Right column =
  self-consistency ECE on $n=2{,}415$ reruns (3-rerun protocol at
  $T=0.7$). All five models exceed $\text{ECE}=0.6$ on
  self-consistency, indicating severe overconfidence; the gap
  between the two columns is direct evidence that stated confidence
  does not reflect actual response stability.}
\end{table}

Table~\ref{tab:calibration-paired} reframes the story. Verbalized
confidence is what a user sees: Claude and GPT-4.1 look
well-calibrated, matching the per-model summary. Self-consistency
asks instead how often the model agrees with itself across reruns,
and the picture inverts: every model crosses the conventional
$\text{ECE} = 0.6$ ``severe overconfidence'' threshold and the
cross-model spread collapses. A model that states $90\%$ confidence
but reproduces its own answer only $60\%$ of the time is calibrated
only verbally. Verbalized confidence is pattern matching on
calibration-shaped language, while self-consistency probes underlying
stability, and the two come apart by an order of magnitude. The
self-consistency ECE treats single-answer agreement across reruns
as ground truth, excluding cases where all three reruns fail to
produce a parseable answer ($n = 2{,}415$).

\subsection{Extended Diagnostics: Where the Divergence Lives}
\label{sec:extended-diagnostics}

The \S\ref{sec:headline-divergence} headline divergence and the
\S\ref{sec:assumption-compliance}/\S\ref{sec:failure-taxonomy}
failure analyses are reported as model-level summaries. The
following subsections decompose those summaries to reveal where the
divergence concentrates: which models contribute disproportionately
to the keyword-judge disagreement, which task types absorb the
failures, which perturbation axes drive the robustness gap, and how
the closed-form and computational phases differ.

\subsubsection{Per-Model Disagreement Profiles}
\label{sec:per-model-disagreement}

The pooled $4.5\times$ asymmetry in \S\ref{sec:assumption-compliance}
(Table~\ref{tab:krippendorff-strip}) decomposes unevenly across the
five models. Mistral has the most lopsided profile: 109 keyword-PASS
/ judge-FAIL versus only 9 in the reverse direction, a $12.1\times$
asymmetry. Claude is second at $7.84\times$ (149 vs 19), followed
by Gemini ($4.19\times$, 130 vs 31) and DeepSeek ($3.12\times$,
100 vs 32). GPT-4.1 is the most balanced at $2.58\times$ (103 vs
40); its keyword passes are also its judge passes more often than
any other model in the sample. The over-credit problem is not
uniform: two of the five models account for $43.7\%$ of the 591
keyword-PASS / judge-FAIL cases.

This sharpens the GPT-4.1 robustness paradox from
\S\ref{sec:failure-taxonomy} in an unexpected direction. GPT-4.1
has the most overall L1 failures (38 of 143) yet the lowest
keyword-judge asymmetry; its surface tokens track the judge's
substantive read more closely than any other model. The first-place
robustness rank therefore does not reduce to pattern-matching of
assumption-shaped language --- the candidate that did would be
Mistral or Claude. GPT-4.1's robustness instead coincides with
substantively correctable failures: when the keyword rubric passes
it, the judge agrees; when it fails, both agree. Robustness here
reflects within-prompt consistency rather than over-credited surface
form.

\subsubsection{Failure Concentration by Task Type}
\label{sec:failure-by-task-type}

The 143 L1 failures distribute across phases with a counter-intuitive
shape: Phase~1 closed-form tasks absorb all $143$ ($21.03\%$ of $680$
Phase~1 responses) while Phase~2 computational methods (Gibbs, MH,
HMC, RJMCMC, hierarchical, VB, ABC) absorb zero ($0.00\%$ of $175$
responses). Computational sampling is not the failure mode the
benchmarked LLMs hit at the L1 severity tier; closed-form algebra is.
The likely mechanism: Phase~2 prompts invite procedural answers
(``run Gibbs for $N$ iterations'') whose ground-truth tolerance is
$0.05$--$0.10$ and whose assumption articulation is implicit in the
sampler choice, while Phase~1 prompts require explicit prior,
likelihood, and posterior derivation that the judge can audit
line-by-line.

The 143 failures cluster in 18 of 38 task types; 20 task types are
failure-free. The top three are REGRESSION (25), BAYES\_REG (25),
and BOX\_MULLER (19). The first two are particularly stark: every
model fails on every problem of those types ($5\times 5=25$ each).
This is a TASK-difficulty pattern, not a model-shortcut pattern.

This complicates the \S\ref{sec:per-model-disagreement} story rather
than reinforcing it. The pattern-matching champions identified there
(Mistral, Claude) do not dominate the task-type failures: REGRESSION
and BAYES\_REG hit all five models equally, and the bottom of the
top-10 (MSE\_COMPARE, BAYES\_FACTOR, LOG\_ML) is dominated by GPT-4.1
and DeepSeek --- the models with the lowest \S\ref{sec:per-model-disagreement}
asymmetry. Per-model over-credit on assumption-compliance and
per-task-type total L1 failure rate are independent signals.

\subsubsection{Robustness by Perturbation Axis}
\label{sec:per-axis-robustness}

The per-axis pooled means across models invert the prior hypothesis:
rephrasing is the most damaging axis at $R = 0.966$, with semantic
$R = 0.984$ and numerical $R = 0.978$ slightly less damaging.
Rewording prompts hurts more than changing the numbers, indicating
sensitivity to surface phrasing even when the underlying math is
identical. Numerical perturbation runs only $n=530$ (40 conceptual
or seeded-MC tasks skip that axis per \S\ref{sec:methodology}) but
the spread is the widest: $0.9146$ (DeepSeek) to $1.0130$ (Mistral),
a $0.10$-point gap.

Variance decomposition shows robustness is more a model property
than an axis property: mean model-variance within axis is
$7.28 \times 10^{-4}$, mean axis-variance within model is
$1.99 \times 10^{-4}$ --- a $3.7\times$ ratio. Table~I's robustness
column captures a real model signal, not an average over
heterogeneous axis behavior.

Four (model, axis) cells exceed $R = 1.0$, meaning the model scores
higher on perturbed prompts than on their base counterparts:
GPT-4.1 (semantic $1.011$, numerical $1.013$) and Mistral (semantic
$1.006$, numerical $1.013$). GPT-4.1's pooled $0.9996$ is therefore
not uniform stability but a near-cancellation: a rephrasing drop of
$0.021$ offsets the two axes where it gains. Cross-linking
\S\ref{sec:per-model-disagreement}/\S\ref{sec:failure-by-task-type}:
the over-credit ranking (Mistral, Claude) does not align with the
robustness ranking (GPT-4.1, Mistral). Mistral is both the largest
over-credit contributor and the second-most robust --- pattern
matching on surface tokens can be stable across paraphrases even
when it is substantively wrong.

\subsubsection{Closed-Form vs Computational Performance}
\label{sec:per-phase-performance}

Phase~2 computational tasks score $0.030$ NMACR points higher on
average than Phase~1 closed-form tasks (Phase~1 mean $0.6816$,
Phase~2 mean $0.7115$), reversing the ``computational is harder''
prior. The dimension breakdown explains the inversion: Phase~2
assumption-compliance averages $0.826$ versus Phase~1's $0.420$
($+0.41$), while numeric accuracy collapses from $0.590$ to $0.080$
($-0.51$). The NMACR weighting ($A=0.30$ vs $N=0.10$) lets the
assumption gain dominate the numeric loss. Intra-phase rankings
flip substantially: Mistral jumps from $5\to 2$, DeepSeek drops
$3\to 5$, Claude drops $2\to 3$. The phase axis is a fourth
independent rank-divergence dimension beyond accuracy, robustness,
and calibration. Phase~2's zero L1 failure rate
(\S\ref{sec:failure-by-task-type}) is consistent with this picture:
implicit-assumption sampler prompts produce continuous partial
credit on $A$ that the judge accepts and the binary L1 flag does
not trigger.
