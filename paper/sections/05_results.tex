\subsection{Per-Metric Rankings}

The per-model summary can be seen in a table above. Accuracy is the
mean Path~B NMACR score on the 171 base tasks. Robustness $R_m$
is the ratio of mean NMACR score on the $n = 398$ paired
perturbation runs to mean NMACR score on their base counterparts
(Eq.~\ref{eq:robustness}), so $R_m = 1$ corresponds to perfect
base-to-perturbation agreement. ECE is binary-pass Expected
Calibration Error (lower is better).

No two rankings agree. Accuracy reads
Gemini $>$ Claude $>$ GPT-4.1 $>$ DeepSeek $>$ Mistral, with Gemini
three points ahead of Claude. Robustness reads
GPT-4.1 $>$ Mistral $>$ Gemini $>$ Claude $>$ DeepSeek, moving
GPT-4.1 from third to first and Claude from second to fourth. Calibration reads Claude $>$ GPT-4.1 $>$ Gemini $>$ Mistral
$>$ DeepSeek. Claude and GPT-4.1 are tied at the top within the
95\% bootstrap CI (10{,}000 resamples, the 0.0005 gap falls inside
the interval), while DeepSeek's ECE of $0.198$ is roughly six times
the leader's. DeepSeek is the only model in the bottom two on every
axis.

\subsection{Headline Divergence}

Figure~\ref{fig:rank-flow} makes the cross-metric divergence
concrete. GPT-4.1 ranks third on accuracy ($0.673$), behind Gemini
($0.731$) and Claude ($0.698$), yet its base and perturbation mean
NMACR scores fall within $0.0003$ ($0.6661$ vs $0.6658$), the
tightest agreement among the five and first place on robustness
($0.9996$). Claude shows the opposite pattern: second on accuracy
($0.698$), tied with GPT-4.1 at the top of calibration
(ECE $\approx 0.033$), but mean NMACR score falls from $0.7011$ on
base prompts to $0.6706$ on perturbed ones, a three-point drop that
pushes it to fourth on robustness ($0.9565$). A practitioner picking GPT-4.1 for its
robustness accepts a middle-of-the-pack accuracy model. One picking
Claude for accuracy and calibration accepts the second-most-brittle
behavior under perturbation.

\subsection{Assumption-Compliance Results}

On $n=750$ paired runs the Llama 3.3 70B Turbo judge passes only
$55.7\%$ of responses on assumption-compliance, leaving $44.3\%$
that fail to articulate the assumptions the derivation depends on,
even when the final number is right. The keyword rubric passes
$71.1\%$ of the same responses, $15.4$ points higher. Spearman
rank correlation between keyword and judge scores on this dimension
is $\rho = 0.602$; Krippendorff $\alpha = 0.573$ (95\% CI
$[0.516, 0.622]$, 1{,}000-resample bootstrap).

On the combined base-plus-perturbation pool ($n=2850$ eligible
runs), $20.7\%$ of responses pass-flip between the keyword rubric
and the judge: one in five times, the two scorers disagree on
whether the assumption was actually articulated. That is the
clearest evidence in the paper for the title's question. Models
produce surface-correct outputs that pass keyword checks for
``assume'' or ``given'' tokens while failing the substantive check.
Du et al.~\cite{du2025icecream} report the same blindness in causal
reasoning. Figure~\ref{fig:krippendorff-strip} shows the
per-dimension $\alpha$ values from the inter-rater check.

\subsection{Calibration Across Extraction Methods}

A single ECE number hides where the (mis)calibration comes from.
Two extraction methods are separated below. \emph{Verbalized confidence} uses
what the model states alongside its answer. \emph{Self-consistency
confidence} is inferred from how often the model returns the same
answer across reruns at temperature $T = 0.7$.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \resizebox{\columnwidth}{!}{%
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Model} & \textbf{Verbalized ECE $\downarrow$}
                   & \textbf{Self-consistency ECE $\downarrow$} \\
                   & {\footnotesize $n=855$ base runs}
                   & {\footnotesize $n=2{,}415$ reruns, $T=0.7$} \\
    \midrule
    Claude    & 0.033 & 0.734 \\
    GPT-4.1   & 0.034 & 0.721 \\
    Gemini    & 0.077 & 0.618 \\
    Mistral   & 0.081 & 0.663 \\
    DeepSeek  & 0.198 & 0.726 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}%
  }
  \caption{Calibration measured by two extraction methods.
           \emph{Left column:} verbalized ECE on $n=855$ truly-base
           runs (171 base tasks $\times$ 5 models). Claude (0.033)
           and GPT-4.1 (0.034) look well-calibrated, with DeepSeek
           (0.198) the clear outlier. \emph{Right column:}
           self-consistency ECE on $n=2{,}415$ reruns (3-rerun
           protocol at $T=0.7$). All five models exceed
           $\text{ECE}=0.6$, indicating severe overconfidence. The
           gap is direct evidence that stated confidence does not
           reflect actual response stability.}
  \label{fig:calibration-paired}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:calibration-paired} reframes the story. Verbalized
confidence is what a user sees: Claude and GPT-4.1 look
well-calibrated, matching the per-model summary. Self-consistency
asks instead how often the model agrees with itself across reruns,
and the picture inverts: every model crosses the conventional
$\text{ECE} = 0.6$ ``severe overconfidence'' threshold and the
cross-model spread collapses. A model that states $90\%$ confidence
but reproduces its own answer only $60\%$ of the time is calibrated
only verbally. Verbalized confidence is pattern matching on
calibration-shaped language, while self-consistency probes underlying
stability, and the two come apart by an order of magnitude. The
self-consistency ECE treats single-answer agreement across reruns
as ground truth, excluding cases where all three reruns fail to
produce a parseable answer ($n = 2{,}415$).
