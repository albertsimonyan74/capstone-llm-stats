\begin{abstract}
Accuracy-only leaderboards hide the failure modes that matter when
large language models are used for statistical reasoning. We
benchmark five frontier LLMs (Claude Sonnet 4.5, GPT-4.1, Gemini
2.5 Flash, DeepSeek-Chat, and Mistral Large) on 171 Bayesian and
inferential tasks across 38 task types, with 473 perturbations
along three axes: rephrase, semantic, and numerical. Each response
is scored twice, once by a deterministic keyword rubric and once by
an out-of-family judge, Llama 3.3 70B Turbo via Together AI, that
rates assumption-compliance and reasoning quality. The rankings
disagree. The model with the lowest accuracy (GPT-4.1, 0.621) tops
the robustness ranking with 0.9996. The model that leads both
accuracy (Claude, 0.683) and binary-pass calibration (Claude, ECE
0.0334) drops to fourth on robustness (0.9565). 44.3\% of responses
do not state the statistical assumptions the derivation depends on,
and assumption violation accounts for 47\% of judge-classified
failures. Keyword and judge agree only moderately on
assumption-compliance (Spearman 0.602) and barely at all on the
other two judged dimensions. A single-number leaderboard is the
wrong instrument for this domain; the model that wins depends on
the metric.
\end{abstract}
