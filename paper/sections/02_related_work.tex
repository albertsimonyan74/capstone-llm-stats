\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Quantitative-reasoning benchmarks.}
StatEval~\cite{lu2025stateval} contains 13{,}817 problems across
several dimensions, including descriptive statistics, probability,
inference, and regression, and a mix of answer correctness and
rubric-based proof evaluation serves as a scoring method.
MathEval~\cite{liu2025matheval} compares 52 LLMs across 22
mathematical-reasoning datasets, again on accuracy. Nagarkar et
al.~\cite{nagarkar2026canllm} compare the responses of LLMs to
humans on statistical tasks. All three mainly take into account how
often the model derives the right number. None of these papers asks
whether the reasoning is well-formed or stable under perturbation.

\textbf{Bayesian reasoning.} Longjohn et
al.~\cite{longjohn2025bayesian} aggregate benchmark results
probabilistically rather than evaluating Bayesian reasoning itself.
Du et al.~\cite{du2025icecream} document assumption-blindness in
causal reasoning: LLMs produce confident causal claims that semantic
cues drive even when the data contradict them. The $44.3\%$ missing
assumption rate reported in this research extends the pattern to
inferential statistics.

\textbf{Calibration.} Guo et al.~\cite{guo2017calibration}
introduced Expected Calibration Error and showed that modern neural
networks, despite being high on accuracy, are, in fact,
systematically miscalibrated. For classifiers, ECE is well studied.
Applying it to free-form LLM reasoning is recent and less settled.
FermiEval and related
work~\cite{fermieval2025,multianswer2026,nagarkar2026canllm} usually
adapt calibration to natural-language outputs on a single dimension.
Binary-pass ECE and a per-NMACR-dimension ECE are reported in this
research.

\textbf{LLM-as-judge.} Zheng et al.~\cite{zheng2023judge} introduced
LLM as a judge with MT-Bench, showing that strong LLMs can
substitute for human raters on open-ended tasks. Follow-up work
documents design-choice sensitivity and rubric-adherence
failures~\cite{park2025judge,judgment2025noise}.
Brittlebench~\cite{brittlebench2026} documents prompt sensitivity.
ReasonBench~\cite{reasonbench2025} documents run-to-run instability
from stochastic decoding. The protocol used here constrains the
judge to a single dimension (assumption-compliance) and uses an
out-of-family open-weights model (Llama 3.3 70B Turbo). Feuer et
al.~\cite{judgment2025noise} address both choices of failure modes.

No prior benchmark is known for Bayesian or inferential statistical
reasoning that combines (i) a perturbation taxonomy for robustness,
(ii) an external single-dimension judge for assumption-compliance,
and (iii) calibration measured on the same task set. The
contribution of this work is the combination of those three, which
surfaces ranking divergences that a single-metric leaderboard
cannot.
