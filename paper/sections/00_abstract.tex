\begin{abstract}
Large language models are increasingly applied to quantitative and
statistical reasoning, yet accuracy-only leaderboards obscure failure
modes that matter in practice. We benchmark five frontier LLMs (Claude
Sonnet 4.5, GPT-4.1, Gemini 2.5 Flash, DeepSeek-Chat, and Mistral Large)
on 171 Bayesian and inferential tasks spanning 38 task types, plus 473
principled perturbations across three axes (rephrase, semantic, and
numerical). Each response is scored by both a deterministic keyword
rubric and an external out-of-family judge, Llama 3.3 70B Turbo via
Together AI, which independently rates assumption-compliance and
reasoning quality. Rankings of the same five models reorder substantially
when the evaluation axis changes. The model worst on accuracy (ChatGPT,
0.621) is best on robustness (0.9996), an essentially perfect score
under perturbation. The model leading on accuracy and on binary-pass
calibration (Claude, accuracy 0.683, ECE 0.0334) ranks fourth of five on
robustness (0.9565). Independent of ranking, 44.3 percent of responses
fail to explicitly state the statistical assumptions their derivations
require, and assumption violation is the dominant failure mode (47
percent of judge-classified failures). Keyword and external-judge
agreement on assumption-compliance is moderate (Spearman 0.602) and far
weaker on the other two judged dimensions. We argue that single-number
leaderboards are insufficient for statistical-reasoning evaluation and
that the right ranking depends on the property being measured.
\end{abstract}
