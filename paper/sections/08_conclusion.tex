\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Five frontier LLMs were benchmarked on 171 Bayesian and inferential
tasks with 473 perturbations, scored by a keyword rubric, an NMACR
aggregator with literature-derived weights, and an out-of-family
Llama 3.3 70B Turbo judge constrained to assumption-compliance. The
five models produce three different rank orders: GPT-4.1, third on
accuracy ($0.673$), is most robust ($0.9996$). Claude, second on
accuracy ($0.698$) and top on calibration (ECE $0.0334$), drops to
fourth on robustness ($0.9565$).
$44.3\%$ of responses do not articulate the required assumptions,
and the keyword rubric overcounts assumption articulation by
$15.4$ points relative to the judge. A single number is the wrong
instrument for statistical-reasoning evaluation.

\textbf{Threats to validity.} The Llama 3.3 70B Turbo judge can
carry training biases. It is constrained to one dimension and
validated against the keyword rubric (Spearman $\rho = 0.602$).
Krippendorff's $\alpha$ sits in the ``questionable'' range,
reflecting genuine ambiguity in scoring free-form Bayesian reasoning.
The three-ranking divergence is a within-model cross-metric finding
independent of inter-rater agreement. Task coverage is broad for
Bayesian and inferential reasoning but not representative of
statistical reasoning generally. Causal inference and experimental
design are out of scope. Model versions are May 2026 snapshots and
inversion patterns may not replicate as APIs evolve.

\textbf{Future work.} Three extensions follow. First, broaden the
task suite into causal inference, experimental design, and
frequentist hypothesis testing to test whether the three-axis
divergence generalizes. Second, replicate with a second out-of-family
judge to bound judge-bias variance and upper-bound inter-rater
reliability on assumption-compliance. Third, treat the GPT-4.1
inversion as a hypothesis about training-data composition and
decoding choices to be tested in controlled provider-level
experiments.
