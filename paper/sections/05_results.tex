\subsection{Per-Metric Rankings}

Table~\ref{tab:rankings} gives the per-model summary. Accuracy is
the Path~B NMACR-weighted pass rate on the 171 base tasks.
Robustness is one minus the perturbation pass-flip rate on the
473 perturbations. ECE is binary-pass Expected Calibration Error
(lower is better).

No two rankings agree. The accuracy ranking is
Claude $>$ Mistral $>$ Gemini $>$ DeepSeek $>$ GPT-4.1, with
Claude four percentage points ahead of the runner-up and GPT-4.1
last. The robustness ranking is
GPT-4.1 $>$ Mistral $>$ Gemini $>$ Claude $>$ DeepSeek, which
moves GPT-4.1 from last to first and Claude from first to fourth.
The calibration ranking is
Claude $>$ GPT-4.1 $>$ Gemini $>$ Mistral $>$ DeepSeek; Claude
and GPT-4.1 are tied at the top to within the 95\% bootstrap CI
(10{,}000 resamples; the 0.0005 gap falls inside the interval),
while DeepSeek's ECE of $0.198$ is roughly six times the leader's.
DeepSeek is the only model that finishes in the bottom two on
every axis.

\subsection{Headline Divergence}

Figure~\ref{fig:rank-flow} shows the rank divergence. Two
examples carry the headline. \textbf{Example 1 (GPT-4.1
inversion).} The model that finishes last on accuracy (GPT-4.1,
$0.621$) finishes first on robustness ($0.9996$); its base and
perturbation pass rates are $0.6661$ and $0.6658$, a gap of
$0.0003$. \textbf{Example 2 (Claude pattern).} The model that
leads on accuracy ($0.683$) and ties for the lead on calibration
(ECE $0.0334$) drops to fourth on robustness ($0.9565$); its base
pass rate is $0.7011$ and its perturbed pass rate is $0.6706$.
Neither pattern shows up in an accuracy-only ranking. Both
configurations (high accuracy with low robustness, low accuracy
with high robustness) are usually treated as theoretical edge
cases; in this five-model sample they are simultaneously real. A
consumer who picks GPT-4.1 for its robustness score is picking the
model with the lowest base accuracy. A consumer who picks Claude
for its accuracy and calibration is picking the third-most-brittle
of the five.

\subsection{Assumption-Compliance Results}

The Llama 3.3 70B Turbo judge scored each (task, response) pair on
whether the model explicitly stated the assumptions the task
requires. On $n=750$ paired runs the judge passes only $55.7\%$ of
responses on assumption-compliance. That is $44.3\%$ of responses
that fail to articulate the assumptions the derivation depends on,
even when the final number is right. The keyword rubric passes
$71.1\%$ of the same responses, $15.4$ percentage points higher.
Spearman rank correlation between keyword and judge scores on
this dimension is $\rho = 0.602$ (95\% CI $[0.516, 0.622]$ via
Krippendorff bootstrap).

On the combined base-plus-perturbation pool ($n=2850$ eligible
runs), $20.7\%$ of responses pass-flip between the keyword rubric
and the judge: one in five times, the two scorers disagree on
whether the assumption was actually articulated. That gap is the
clearest evidence in the paper for the title's question. Models
produce surface-correct outputs that pass keyword checks for
``assume'' or ``given'' tokens while failing the substantive
check. Du et al.~\cite{du2025icecream} report the same
assumption-blindness in causal reasoning.

\subsection{Calibration Across Extraction Methods}

A single ECE number hides where the (mis)calibration comes from.
We separate two extraction methods. \emph{Verbalized confidence}
uses what the model states alongside its answer. \emph{Self-
consistency confidence} is inferred from how often the model
returns the same answer across reruns at temperature $T = 0.7$.
Inter-rater agreement on the rubric dimensions, summarized in
Figure~\ref{fig:krippendorff-strip}, sits in the conventionally
``questionable'' range, which informs how we read the results.

Figure~\ref{fig:calibration-paired} reframes the calibration story.
Verbalized confidence is what a user sees when interacting with the
model: Claude and GPT-4.1 look well-calibrated by this measure, and
Table~\ref{tab:rankings} reflects that. Self-consistency confidence
asks instead how often the model agrees with itself on the same
prompt across reruns at $T = 0.7$, and the picture inverts. Every
model crosses the conventional $\text{ECE} = 0.6$ threshold for
``severe overconfidence'' and the cross-model spread collapses. A
model that says ``I am $90\%$ sure'' but reproduces its own answer
only $60\%$ of the time is calibrated only verbally; the verbal
claim is detached from the empirical answer distribution. That is
the divergence the title names: verbalized confidence is pattern
matching on calibration-shaped language, self-consistency probes
whether the reasoning underneath is stable, and the two come apart
by an order of magnitude. One methodological choice we should
disclose: the self-consistency ECE treats single-answer agreement
across reruns as the ground-truth signal, which excludes the rare
cases where all three reruns fail to produce a parseable answer
line. Those cases sit in the run logs but are not in the
$n = 2{,}415$ figure.
