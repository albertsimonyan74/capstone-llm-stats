\section{Analysis and Validation}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{What the three-ranking divergence means.} The three metrics
are not the same axis under different names. Accuracy asks whether
the model produces the right number for the prompt as worded.
Robustness asks whether it still produces the right number when the
prompt is reworded, reframed, or has its numbers swapped. Calibration
asks whether stated confidence tracks empirical correctness. Each
has deployment consequences the others do not capture, so the choice
of which metric to report picks the winner. For one-shot question
answering, accuracy is the natural signal. For applications that
paraphrase the prompt or swap in equivalent numerical setups
(automated tutoring, prompt-engineered pipelines, agentic workflows
that write their own prompts), robustness matters more, and the
GPT-4.1 inversion is the warning sign: the accuracy-best model can
be the wrong default. For systems that gate decisions on expressed
confidence (triage tools, ensemble selectors), calibration matters,
and DeepSeek's ECE of $0.198$ rules it out regardless of where it
sits on accuracy.

\textbf{Reasoning versus pattern matching.} The clearest evidence
for the paper's title is the keyword-vs-judge gap on
assumption-compliance. On the combined base-and-perturbation pool
($n = 2850$), $20.7\%$ of responses pass-flip between the two
scorers: one in five times, the keyword check approves a response
the judge rejects, or vice versa, on whether the required assumption
was actually articulated. The judge's lower pass rate ($55.7\%$ vs
the rubric's $71.1\%$) indicates which way the bias runs: surface-form
scoring overcounts substantive assumption articulation. That matters
because assumption articulation is not a stylistic choice in
Bayesian reasoning. Posterior derivations are wrong if the
assumptions are wrong, and a model that lands on the right number
while quietly assuming exchangeability where the task posits
hierarchical structure has solved a different problem than the one
asked. Du et al.~\cite{du2025icecream} report the same blindness in
causal reasoning. The calibration analysis
(Figure~\ref{fig:calibration-paired}) shows the same shape:
verbalized confidence places Claude and GPT-4.1 in the
well-calibrated tier (ECE $\approx 0.033$), while self-consistency
confidence on three reruns at $T = 0.7$ puts every model past the
$\text{ECE} = 0.6$ ``severe overconfidence'' threshold. Verbalized
confidence is pattern matching on calibration-shaped language;
self-consistency probes the underlying answer distribution. Future
benchmarks for statistical reasoning should score
assumption-compliance with a non-self-referential judge and report
calibration under both verbalized and self-consistency extractors,
not under a keyword rubric alone.

\textbf{Broader implications.} The multi-axis ranking is cheap to
add. The same response corpus that feeds an accuracy leaderboard
feeds a robustness leaderboard once perturbations exist, and a
calibration leaderboard once confidence is parsed. The GPT-4.1
accuracy-to-robustness inversion hints that training-data composition
and decoding choices may trade off ``follows the textbook'' against
``answers the question that was actually asked.'' Settling that
would take controlled experiments at the provider level, not
black-box benchmarking. The open audit trail is also a contribution:
every scoring weight, tolerance threshold, and aggregation choice
is documented and linked to its motivating paper, so replication or
reweighting takes a one-line change instead of reverse engineering.
Single-number leaderboards remain useful shorthand. They should be
reported alongside the multi-axis view, not in place of it.
