\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Increased use of large language models as informal statistical
assistants is evident in the fact that most students reach for
them for posterior derivation, practitioners are inquiring sketches
for hierarchical models, and reviewers consult with them on the
assumption articulation behind the testing. Whether they are fit for
these roles is discussed and answered in single-accuracy metric
leaderboards across dozens of research online. Single metric
evaluation is convenient, but it fails in the case of three
properties that are of great concern to a careful user, who prefers
separate explanations for them. The first issue is whether the model
produces the correct value when there is a clean problem statement.
The second issue is that after rephrasing, the model continues to
derive the correct value, even when the problem is recast into a
different domain, or is embodied with new inputs. The third issue
is whether the empirical correctness of the model corresponds to its
stated confidence. Prior LLM-statistics work informs this evaluation
pipeline differently along each axis. Accuracy-focused benchmarks
like MathEval~\cite{liu2025matheval} and the LLM-vs-human study of
Nagarkar et al.~\cite{nagarkar2026canllm} score the first property
exclusively. StatEval~\cite{lu2025stateval} adds proof-evaluation
rubrics but does not measure robustness or calibration. Longjohn et
al.~\cite{longjohn2025bayesian} treat the evaluation itself
probabilistically rather than the reasoning content. A single-column
leaderboard still picks a winner, even when there is a strong
disagreement on the three properties of the same set of models.

The novelty and contribution of this work is an evaluation pipeline
that is designed to independently measure and compare the three
properties. The starting point of the pipeline is a task set of 171
Bayesian and inferential problems across 38 topics, covering both
closed-form conjugate inference and computational methods, such as
Gibbs sampling, Metropolis-Hastings, Hamiltonian Monte Carlo,
Reversible-Jump MCMC, hierarchical models, Variational Bayes, and
Approximate Bayesian Computation. Based on the standard
textbooks~\cite{bolstad2007bayesian,hoff2009bayesian,bishop2006prml},
each task is paired with a ground-truth solver, and the solutions
are not visible or accessible to LLMs in any form. The perturbation
of a task is done along three axes: rephrasing (same math, reworded
prompt), semantic (same math, different real-world context), and
numerical (changed math inputs with recomputed ground-truth),
summing up to total 473 perturbation records. Both the base tasks
and perturbations are simultaneously dispatched to five models
(Claude Sonnet 4.5, GPT-4.1, Gemini 2.5 Flash, DeepSeek-Chat, and
Mistral Large) through a unified HTTP client, which takes records of
latency, token counts, and raw output. Scoring of each response is
done along three paths: a deterministic regex extractor for the
final numeric answer, a five-dimensional NMACR rubric over Numeric
Accuracy, Method Structure, Assumption Compliance, Confidence
Calibration, and Reasoning Quality, and an out-of-family (totally
independent from the five LLMs) judge --- Llama 3.3 70B Turbo, via
Together AI --- which re-scores the three qualitative dimensions.
The aforementioned scoring paths are fed to three separate
aggregations, which then produce three rankings instead of one. The
single-judge protocol has become the default in the literature, so
the dual scoring step deserves a brief justification. Despite being
cheap and reproducible, the keyword rubric confuses surface with
core substance. That is, a response containing the word ``assume''
does not necessarily categorize as a response with a usable
assumption. The retrieved baseline
information~\cite{park2025judge,judgment2025noise} states that a
single LLM judge can read the substance, but exhibits self-preference
and design-choice sensitivity. To convert that methodological tension
into a measurable quantity, both graders are run on each response.
The score is defensible when the two graders agree, that is, give a
PASS based on their thresholds. The disagreement gap itself is
recorded as a finding rather than whitewashed and treated as
evidence that there is a high chance of overestimation of Bayesian
competence in the keyword-only protocols used
earlier~\cite{lu2025stateval,liu2025matheval}.

Contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item 171 Bayesian and inferential tasks are benchmarked (136
        closed-form conjugate models, 35 computational methods
        including Gibbs, Metropolis-Hastings, HMC, RJMCMC,
        hierarchical, VB, ABC), with three types of perturbations
        for all 38 task types.
  \item Deterministic keyword rubric is cross-evaluated with
        independent and open weights judge --- Llama 3.3 70B Turbo.
        Agreement rates are reported per dimension and the
        disagreement gap between the rubric and the judge is itself
        a contributing finding.
  \item Empirical evidence that the accuracy, robustness, and
        calibration rankings of the same five LLMs disagree
        essentially. On a single-metric leaderboard, neither
        aforementioned pattern appears.
  \item Documented and reproducible methodology. NMACR weights
        (A=0.30, R=0.25, M=0.20, C=0.15, N=0.10), aggregation paths,
        and tolerance thresholds are specified inline in \S\ref{sec:methodology}
        and motivated from the cited literature.
\end{enumerate}
